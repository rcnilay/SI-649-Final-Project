The Manager Bounce

In football, firing a manager is always a gamble. It disrupts training, unsettles the squad, and costs money. Yet clubs do it constantly — betting that the shock of a new voice and a fresh set of eyes will arrest a slide and revive the team. This phenomenon has a name: the manager bounce.

The bounce is contentious. Critics argue it is little more than statistical noise — a struggling team regressing to the mean regardless of who is in the dugout. Proponents point to real examples: Spurs under Pochettino, Leicester under Ranieri, Liverpool under Klopp. But what does the data actually show across eleven full Premier League seasons?

To answer that question, we tracked every managerial change in the Premier League from 2014 to 2026 — 159 changes in total — and measured how each team's points-per-game changed in the matches immediately surrounding the switch. We start with one of the most vivid examples of the bounce the era has produced.

A Case Study: Lampard to Tuchel

By January 2021, Frank Lampard's Chelsea were in crisis. A summer transfer window that cost the club over £200 million by bringing in Timo Werner, Kai Havertz, and Ben Chilwell, had not translated into results. A run of one win in five left Chelsea ninth in the table, and owner Roman Abramovich, who had already fired eleven managers before Lampard, had seen enough.

The replacement was Thomas Tuchel, dismissed by Paris Saint-Germain just weeks earlier despite reaching the Champions League final. Tuchel had a reputation for defensive organization and intense tactical preparation — a sharp contrast to Lampard's more open, attacking approach.

The shift is stark. Lampard's final ten league games were erratic — heavy defeats against Manchester City and Arsenal sandwiched between victories, with no consistent pattern to suggest a side settling into a system. Under Tuchel, Chelsea immediately became difficult to beat. They conceded almost nothing in his opening weeks, climbing the table steadily before ultimately lifting the Champions League trophy in May.

It is exactly the kind of story that makes the case for the bounce. But Chelsea in 2021 had unusual circumstances: a world-class squad underperforming its talent level, a new manager arriving with a clear tactical identity, and a transfer window's worth of players who had not yet clicked under their previous coach. How often does football hand a new manager such favourable conditions?

Does the bounce exist?

To find out, we expand the lens to every qualifying managerial change across the dataset. For each handover, we calculate the difference in points per game between the outgoing manager's final games and the new manager's first games, using the same window on both sides. A positive number means the team improved; negative means it got worse.

The distribution below is the result. Each bar represents a cluster of manager changes that produced a similar outcome. If the bounce were a universal truth, we would expect the entire distribution to sit clearly to the right of zero. If it were pure noise, the distribution would be centered on zero.

The data tells a murkier story than either camp in the debate would like. The median is positive — more manager changes produced an improvement than a decline — but the effect is modest, and the spread is wide. Plenty of clubs fired their manager and got worse. The distribution is not centred on zero, but it is not dramatically skewed right either.

Notice too what happens as you widen the window using the slider. With only five games on each side, the bounce looks more pronounced — small samples are volatile, and the results right after a change tend to be the easiest wins. As the window grows to ten or fifteen matches, the distribution compresses. New managers who looked transformative in their first weeks often settle back toward the level of the team they inherited. The bounce, to the extent it exists, appears to fade.

That is consistent with the regression-to-the-mean hypothesis. Managers are typically dismissed after an unusually poor run — one that is partly bad luck. The team was always going to recover somewhat, regardless of who was in the dugout. The new manager gets the credit; the statistics suggest they may not always deserve it.
