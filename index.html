In football, firing a manager is never just a personnel decision. It is a signal that something is broken, patience has run out, and the club needs a reset.

Fans often call what comes next the manager bounce — the short-term lift a team supposedly gets after a new manager arrives. The idea feels intuitive. A new voice can change the mood, simplify tactics, or push players who had gone flat under the previous coach. But it can also be a story we tell after the fact. Many managers are fired after unusually bad runs, so some improvement may have happened anyway.

This project asks a simple question: does the manager bounce actually show up in the data?

To test that, we looked at Premier League managerial changes from 2014 to 2026 and compared each team's points per game before and after the handover. Instead of relying on one famous example, we align many manager changes around the same match zero and look for the pattern underneath the noise.

Case Study Lampard to Tuchel

Chelsea's 2021 switch from Frank Lampard to Thomas Tuchel is almost the perfect version of the manager-bounce story.

By January, Lampard's Chelsea looked stuck. The club had spent heavily on players like Timo Werner, Kai Havertz, and Ben Chilwell, but the results did not match the talent in the squad. Chelsea were sliding down the table, and the team looked too open, too inconsistent, and short on control.

Tuchel arrived with a very different identity. Within weeks, Chelsea became harder to break down. The change was not subtle. The same squad suddenly looked more organised, more compact, and more confident. This is the kind of example that makes the manager bounce feel real: a talented team underperforming, followed by a new coach who immediately gives them structure.

"But that is also why Chelsea is dangerous as evidence. It is memorable, dramatic, and unusually clean. Most Premier League sackings are messier than this." The limits of one example
Chelsea gives us the story. The rest of the dataset lets us test whether the story holds up. A single example can make the bounce look obvious — but across the league, clubs fire managers for many different reasons: poor tactics, injuries, bad recruitment, dressing-room problems, or simply bad luck. If the manager bounce is more than a myth, we should see improvement across many cases, not just in the ones fans remember.

The Evidence Does the bounce exist?

For each managerial change, we calculated the difference in points per game before and after the handover. A positive value means the team improved after changing managers. A negative value means the team got worse.

The distribution shows that the bounce exists, but not in the clean, dramatic way it is often talked about. Most teams improved after making a change, and the median change is positive. That supports the idea that clubs often get some short-term lift.

But the spread matters. A large number of teams did not improve, and some declined after changing managers. The bounce is not automatic. It is better understood as a tendency than a guarantee.

The window size also changes the story. When we look only a few matches before and after the change, the bounce looks stronger. But as the window expands, the effect becomes smaller and less dramatic. That suggests some of the early lift may be short-lived or partly driven by teams recovering from an unusually bad run.

Over Time The Bounce Trajectory

The distribution tells us whether teams improved overall, but it does not show the shape of the bounce. Does the improvement happen immediately? Does it build slowly? Or does it fade after the first few matches?

The trajectory chart gives a clearer answer. On average, teams do improve shortly after a managerial change. The first few matches under a new manager tend to sit above the pre-change baseline, which suggests that the bounce is not just a fan myth.

But the effect is modest. It is not a total transformation. The average improvement is small, and by around the eighth or ninth match, the advantage starts to fade back toward the team's earlier level.

That fading is important. Managers are usually fired after a poor stretch, and poor stretches often include bad luck as well as bad performance. Some recovery may have happened even without a new manager. In other words, the new manager may deserve some credit, but probably not all of it.

Deeper Beyond the Scoreline

Points per game is the headline number, but it does not explain how a team changed.

A club might collect more points because it genuinely became better: creating more chances, defending more cleanly, or controlling games more consistently. But it might also improve because of finishing luck, weaker opponents, or a few close results going its way.

That is why we also look at performance indicators beyond points. The radar chart compares each team before and after a managerial change across several dimensions, including goals scored, shots on target, corners, clean sheets, goals conceded, and win rate. This view helps show whether a manager change shifted the team's actual playing profile, not just the final score. Some handovers show a clear tactical shift. Others look more like a results bump without a major underlying change.

All 159 Exploring Every Change

The histogram and trajectory chart show us averages and distributions, but they hide the individual stories. Each of those 159 managerial changes is a club, a manager, a set of circumstances. The scatter plot below makes every one of them visible.

Each dot is one manager change. The horizontal axis shows the team's performance before the handover; the vertical axis shows performance after. Dots above the dashed diagonal improved. Dots below it got worse. The immediate takeaway is the spread: there is no tight cluster of points just above the line. The dots are scattered across all four quadrants.

Toggle between points per game and goal differential to see whether the bounce looks different depending on which lens you use. Goal differential tends to be a stricter judge — it is harder to sustain a gap between goals scored and goals conceded than it is to collect points through tight wins. A club that looks better in the points view but similar in the goal differential view may simply have gotten lucky with results.

Select specific clubs from the grid to trace their managerial history. Clubs with serial instability — Watford and Sunderland in this era stand out — show a scatter of dots with no clear pattern: some improved, some declined, and the aggregate effect is roughly zero. The churn itself may be the problem. Stability, it turns out, is its own form of performance. Click any dot to see the full breakdown for that change.

Verdict Conclusion

The manager bounce is real, but it is smaller and less reliable than the phrase makes it sound.

Changing managers can create a short-term lift, especially in the first few matches. But across the Premier League, that lift is uneven, often modest, and likely mixed with regression to the mean. Some clubs genuinely improve under a new manager. Others simply return to normal after a bad run. Some get worse.

"The better question may not be whether the bounce exists. The data suggests that it does. The better question is: when is the bounce actually about the manager, and when is it just football correcting itself?" The real question
