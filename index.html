The Manager Bounce

In football, firing a manager is always a gamble. It disrupts training, unsettles the squad, and costs money. Yet clubs do it constantly — betting that the shock of a new voice and a fresh set of eyes will arrest a slide and revive the team. This phenomenon has a name: the manager bounce.

The bounce is contentious. Critics argue it is little more than statistical noise — a struggling team regressing to the mean regardless of who is in the dugout. Proponents point to real examples: Spurs under Pochettino, Leicester under Ranieri, Liverpool under Klopp. But what does the data actually show across eleven full Premier League seasons?

To answer that question, we tracked every managerial change in the Premier League from 2014 to 2026 — 159 changes in total — and measured how each team's points-per-game changed in the matches immediately surrounding the switch. Only changes with enough games on both sides of the handover were included; caretaker appointments are flagged separately throughout.

We start with one of the most vivid examples of the bounce the era has produced.

A Case Study: Lampard to Tuchel

By January 2021, Frank Lampard's Chelsea were in crisis. A summer transfer window that cost the club over £200 million by bringing in Timo Werner, Kai Havertz, and Ben Chilwell, had not translated into results. A run of one win in five left Chelsea ninth in the table, and owner Roman Abramovich, who had already fired eleven managers before Lampard, had seen enough.

The replacement was Thomas Tuchel, dismissed by Paris Saint-Germain just weeks earlier despite reaching the Champions League final. Tuchel had a reputation for defensive organization and intense tactical preparation — a sharp contrast to Lampard's more open, attacking approach.

The shift is stark. Lampard's final ten league games were erratic — heavy defeats against Manchester City and Arsenal sandwiched between victories, with no consistent pattern to suggest a side settling into a system. Under Tuchel, Chelsea immediately became difficult to beat. They conceded almost nothing in his opening weeks, climbing the table steadily before ultimately lifting the Champions League trophy in May.

What made the turnaround so sudden was not just personnel — the squad barely changed in January — but shape and defensive structure. Tuchel installed a back three almost immediately, compressing Chelsea's defensive block and removing the space behind the full-backs that had been so ruthlessly exposed under Lampard. The results reflected it: just two goals conceded in his first eight Premier League matches.

It is exactly the kind of story that makes the case for the bounce. But Chelsea in 2021 had unusual circumstances: a world-class squad underperforming its talent level, a new manager arriving with a clear tactical identity, and a transfer window's worth of players who had not yet clicked under their previous coach. How often does football hand a new manager such favourable conditions?

Does the bounce exist?

To find out, we expand the lens to every qualifying managerial change across the dataset. For each handover, we calculate the difference in points per game between the outgoing manager's final games and the new manager's first games, using the same window on both sides. A positive number means the team improved; negative means it got worse.

The distribution below is the result. Each bar represents a cluster of manager changes that produced a similar outcome. If the bounce were a universal truth, we would expect the entire distribution to sit clearly to the right of zero. If it were pure noise, the distribution would be centered on zero.

The data tells a murkier story than either camp in the debate would like. The median is positive — more manager changes produced an improvement than a decline — but the effect is modest, and the spread is wide. Plenty of clubs fired their manager and got worse. The distribution is not centred on zero, but it is not dramatically skewed right either.

Notice too what happens as you widen the window using the slider. With only five games on each side, the bounce looks more pronounced — small samples are volatile, and the results right after a change tend to be the easiest wins. As the window grows to ten or fifteen matches, the distribution compresses. New managers who looked transformative in their first weeks often settle back toward the level of the team they inherited. The bounce, to the extent it exists, appears to fade.

That is consistent with the regression-to-the-mean hypothesis. Managers are typically dismissed after an unusually poor run — one that is partly bad luck. The team was always going to recover somewhat, regardless of who was in the managerial position. The new manager gets the credit; and the statistics suggest they may not always deserve it.

There are, however, genuine outliers — changes that produced large, sustained improvements that are difficult to explain through regression alone. These tend to share a pattern: the incoming manager had a well-defined tactical system, the squad had the personnel to execute it, and the change was made early enough in the season for that system to take root. Claudio Ranieri's Leicester, Jürgen Klopp's early months at Liverpool, and Nuno Espírito Santo's first campaign at Wolves all appear in the rightmost bars of this distribution. They are the exceptions — but they are real.

Equally instructive are the changes that made things worse. A significant minority of appointments — roughly a third at most window sizes — produced teams that performed worse under the new manager than the old one. These are often late-season rescues where the squad is already depleted, or situations where the new manager's style is simply a poor fit for the players available. The hire-and-hope strategy has a measurable failure rate.

Beyond the Scoreline

Points per game captures the result but not the story. A team can improve its points haul while creating fewer chances, or concede more goals while climbing the table through sheer finishing luck. To understand what actually changes when a new manager arrives, we need to look beneath the headline number.

The radar chart below compares six performance dimensions across a ten-match window on each side of every mid-season managerial change in the dataset. Each axis runs from the dataset's worst value at the centre to its best at the edge, so a larger polygon always means better performance. Goals conceded is inverted for the same reason. Select a club and then a specific handover to see how the team's tactical fingerprint shifted.

What the radar reveals most consistently is that offensive metrics respond faster to a managerial change than defensive ones. Shot volumes, corners, and goals scored tend to shift within the first five or six games — the new manager's pressing triggers and attacking patterns are the first things that show up in the data. Defensive solidity, measured here through clean sheets and goals conceded, takes longer to build. A new back-line shape requires repetition on the training ground, and the results reflect it: many managers show early offensive improvement but a lag before the defensive numbers catch up.

This asymmetry matters for interpreting early results under a new appointment. A team that wins its first three games under a new manager may be doing so on improved attacking output while still conceding at the same rate as before — a profile that becomes unsustainable as opponents adjust. The radar makes these mixed pictures visible in a way that the points tally alone cannot.

Some handovers tell a more coherent story. When both polygons move outward together — more goals, fewer conceded, better win rate — it is a sign of genuine structural improvement rather than a temporary lift. When the after polygon is larger on some axes but smaller on others, the picture is mixed: the new manager changed the team's identity without necessarily improving its results. These are often the appointments that look promising early and then plateau.

The churn itself may be the problem. Stability, it turns out, is its own form of performance.

What the Data Shows

The manager bounce is real, but it is smaller, more variable, and more fleeting than the cycle of sackings and new appointments would suggest. Across 159 changes, the median improvement is positive — slightly more clubs got better than got worse — but the spread is wide and the effect shrinks the longer you look. By fifteen games under a new manager, the performance gap has typically compressed to something close to statistical noise.

That compression is the strongest evidence for the regression-to-the-mean argument. Teams are fired when they are performing below their talent level; they were going to recover somewhat anyway. The new manager arrives, the squad responds to the novelty of fresh instructions, and results improve. Then opposition analysts adapt, early-season luck averages out, and the team settles back toward what its underlying quality was always likely to produce. The manager gets credit for the improvement and blame for the plateau — neither entirely deserved.

What separates the genuine bounces from the illusory ones is harder to read in the data but visible in the radar charts: coherence. The appointments that produced lasting improvement were ones where the new manager had a clear system, the squad had the players to run it, and there was enough of the season remaining for that system to become automatic. These conditions are rarely all present at once, which is why the transformative bounce — the Klopp at Liverpool, the Tuchel at Chelsea — remains the exception rather than the rule.

For clubs deciding whether to make a change, the data offers a sober caution. The expected return on a managerial sacking is modest at best and negative at worst. The decision is rarely purely about performance; it involves dressing-room dynamics, board confidence, and commercial pressures that no dataset can fully capture. But when the question is simply whether firing the manager tends to make the football better, the honest answer is: sometimes, a little, and not for long.
